Introduction

Update: 2026-07-06

An English version of the whole document is planned. This Introduction and the Executive Summary are written in English. Case 01–10 Analysis and the Open Questions Map remain in Korean for now. Full case documentation maintained separately.

1 About This Portfolio

This portfolio analyzes how judgment evolved while providing Korean-domain expertise as a contractor on an AI lab project. The focus is not each case’s deliverable but the mechanism by which judgment forms in the absence of official guidelines to apply. Each case records criteria built during annotation work and shows how those criteria repeated, shifted and developed across the ten cases. In this portfolio, the Reasoner refers to the author who forms and evolves the judgment, a term used to distinguish this role from the general annotator role on the project.

2 Structure

2.1 Executive Summary

The Executive Summary is a synthesis written after all ten cases were completed. It covers the recurring mechanism across cases and the trajectory of shifting judgment criteria, neither of which is visible from within a single case. The reading order aligns with a top-down approach, placing macro patterns first and individual cases subsequently.

2.2 Case File Structure

Each case file has four sections.

Section 1 (Case Overview) functions as an index. It contains:

  • Case Summary: the subject of judgment and the sequential reasoning flow
  • Key Findings: the judgment criteria or structural discoveries the case revealed
  • Connection Axes: the specific type of problem (e.g. Localization Safety Taxonomy) each case’s judgment addresses
  • Case Rating: the case value based on its strength as empirical evidence

This section alone should let a reader decide whether to read the rest.

Section 2 (Case Analysis) covers how the judgment formed and evolved during the analysis:

  • Reasoning Architecture: the judgment structure layer by layer
  • Key Evidence: internal evidence (derived from the Reasoner’s own reasoning) kept separate from external evidence (standard sources)
  • Case Limitations: the practical limitations encountered during the reasoning process, including the mitigation applied and what remains unresolved
  • References: external sources listed in a fixed hierarchy

Section 3 (Analytical Reflection) looks back at Section 2’s judgment from outside the case:

  • Calibration: how judgment criteria shifted from the previous case
  • Criteria Development: this case’s place in the portfolio and what carries forward to the next
  • Observation: design-level findings included only when applicable
  • Boundaries: the range this case cannot verify

Section 4 (Broader Context & Open Ends) looks beyond the case itself:

  • Domain Substrate Context: the structural context arising from Korean-language processing
  • Physical AI Implications: possible extension to Physical AI, kept at the level of speculation
  • Open Questions: questions this portfolio cannot answer

2.3 Open Questions Map

Open Questions Map tracks how open questions discovered across the ten cases connect to one another, and to what extent those questions have been resolved.

3 What This Portfolio Is Not

This portfolio does not address the following.

  • Linguistic analysis or academic contribution: the judgments within each case are not meant to establish academic validity.
  • Evaluation within Korean speech-processing scholarship: the work draws on judgment criteria formed during transcription and annotation, but does not assess those criteria against linguistic or phonetic scholarship.
  • Engineering validation: the connections drawn in Section 4.2 (Physical AI Implications) stay at the level of speculation. Whether that speculation holds up technically in actual robotics or embedded systems is not verified here.
  • Full reproduction of the speech moment: what this portfolio preserves is the reproducibility of judgment criteria, not the conditions of the speech moment itself (intonation, speaker intent, context).
  • Internal work guidelines or raw case data: it does not quote or describe in detail the internal guidelines referenced during annotation, or the actual utterances and responses processed. It addresses the judgment criteria formed where those guidelines fall short.
