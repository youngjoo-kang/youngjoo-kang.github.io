Executive Summary

Case 01–10 Utterance Project Portfolio, Meta-Analysis

1 About This Document

This document was synthesized after completing the analysis of the ten cases, drawing on the accumulated Calibration Notes (recording how judgment criteria shifted from the previous case) and Open Questions. This is not a word-for-word preservation of the original working process. For data integrity, two rounds of verification against the original case files identified and corrected discrepancies. These included mistakes in recording connections between cases, omissions where an item was dropped from the final list, and items where a connection between cases never actually formed, requiring relocation from the case-level narrative (Section 3) to the portfolio-scope questions (Section 4).

2 A Recurring Mechanism

Looking at how the overall judgment evolved across the ten cases, the target domains (e.g. lexical function, orthographic branching, loanword treatment) differ each time, but the underlying mechanism by which judgment forms consistently recurs through an identical four stages.

  1. Apply an existing criterion: each case begins by applying the annotation guideline, dictionary orthographies, or a processing criterion the Reasoner has already formed, onto the target vocabulary.
  2. Identify the criterion’s limit: the point where that criterion fails to resolve the case: either the criterion does not address it at all (a gap), addresses it without settling on one answer (multiple answers allowed), or every existing criterion has been tried and none fits (exhaustion).
  3. Form a provisional criterion: a new scenario or priority principle is built directly to fill the point where the existing criteria stalled. At this stage a verification principle is usually specified alongside it.
  4. Formalize as an independent criterion: the criterion is formalized as a general principle extending beyond this case, while at the same time stating its limits or asymmetries in the same place.

These four stages are not simply a repeated narrative form. Although the nature of the blocking point differed every time (a plain omission in the guideline, a categorical gap in the hierarchy, an error in judgment order, conservatism in the evidence path, the openness of the external norm the hierarchy itself depends on), the skeleton of the procedure for handling that point never changed. This is not formal consistency, an incidental repetition of style, but substantive consistency: the same procedure resolving different problems each time.

This mechanism has been repeatedly confirmed within the ten cases of the Utterance domain (spoken Korean data). Whether it generalizes across other domains (other annotation projects the author plans to undertake) requires separate verification against patterns from other projects.

3 Carried Forward

3.1 The Shifting Baseline

A. Calibration Tracking

Reading the Calibration Notes in case order, both the judgment criteria and the unit of ambiguity shift. ‘Section 3.1 Calibration’ of each case file, which logs how the criteria shifted relative to the prior case, reveals that each transition was assessed on whether it extended the prior trajectory or departed from it. That assessment loop was highly iterative; a provisional criterion established in one case would either be carried forward or deferred into the next case.

While Case 01 was a boundary judgment over which classification an individual spoken word belonged to, and Case 02 was a recognition of hierarchy (which judgment criterion takes precedence over another), Case 03 exposed a gap that the hierarchy of judgment criteria had not been internalized in advance. In Case 04, the issue shifted entirely from hierarchical structure to the operational scope of the guideline. Case 05 stepped even further past those boundaries into a gap with no explicit guideline, calling for a self-constructed judgment principle, later verified against the reasoning behind the English guideline’s own examples. Case 06, after systematically applying the hierarchy to exhaustion, found a gap within it and derived a principle from outside the hierarchy. The order in which to apply that derived principle then emerged as the next problem. In Case 07, it became evident that the order of applying several criteria could fundamentally alter the outcome. A principle formed that the validity of the execution order had to be verified independently of the final conclusion. Case 08 confirmed that this principle held not only for order but also for the form of evidence (direct evidence versus indirect evidence drawn from an adjacent level within the hierarchy), even under conditions where the conclusion did not change. Case 09 moved beyond resolving individual judgments. For the first time, it organized how the judgment criteria built separately interlocked with one another. This organizing effort also revealed a gap: no higher-order rule existed for deciding which criterion a new input should be routed to. With that unresolved point still standing, Case 10 took up a stretch where, even following the external standard authoritative source all the way through, no single correct answer could be found.

The unit of ambiguity evolved through distinct conceptual stages. It started from a boundary classification over an individual word, moved to the question of which criterion took precedence over the others, and then grew into the recognition that the hierarchy of judgment criteria as a whole had yet to be internalized. That recognition rose a step higher, into the problem of filling an area where no hierarchy existed at all. That gap was filled with a self-established principle, one verified afterward against a cross-linguistic reference, extending as far as confirming that precision requirements differ across task types. Here the unit of ambiguity shifted to identifying a category the hierarchy could not cover and establishing a new principle outside it. It then became clear that the order of applying that principle could itself alter the outcome. This led to the confirmation that the form of the supporting evidence had to be verified separately from the conclusion it reached, even when that conclusion stayed the same. That confirmation rose to a higher level, into systematizing how the separately built criteria interlocked with each other. That systematizing narrowed to a single finding: no rule existed for deciding where to route a new input. Finally, a separate finding followed: that external authority can itself admit more than one valid answer, leaving some cases with no single answer to settle on. This left two flaws side by side as unresolved points at different levels: one inside the hierarchy, one outside it.

Turning to whether each case extended the prior trajectory or departed from it: setting aside Case 01 as the reference point, Cases 02 and 03 were both judged extensions, though Case 03 also recorded the possibility of a qualitative shift in the Reasoner’s role. Case 04 was the first where a departure came into question. The Reasoner appeared to shift from someone who recognizes structure to someone who designs it, but the determination was left pending. Only when the same dimension repeated in Case 05 was the shift confirmed. Cases 06 through 08 were judged extensions of the trajectory Cases 04 and 05 had established, with the Reasoner having moved from recognizing structure to designing it. What the Reasoner worked on, though, moved from choosing a tool, to applying it in a certain order, to evaluating in hindsight how conservative the evidence path had been. Case 09 extended that accumulated trajectory and, at the same time, marked a qualitative shift on top of it: the first point where the Reasoner moved from operating the hierarchy to defining it. Case 10 extended the trajectory through Case 09 and expanded the Reasoner’s role in a new direction: diagnosing the limits of the external authority the hierarchy depends on. This direction is separate from the one Case 09 opened: Case 09 turned inward, to defining the hierarchy; Case 10 turned outward, to the external authority it depends on.

One point here can be sharpened: Case 10 is not the first case to meet the limits of an external standard authoritative source. Case 04 had already run into one, when the National Institute of Korean Language gave no clear criterion for adopting a neologism. The difference is what happened afterward. In Case 04, the Reasoner formed a new criterion to fill the gap, and the case was resolved. That limit became the starting point for a new basis of judgment. In Case 10, there was no way past the limit, so the determination was left pending; the limit became the direct cause of the unresolved state. The accurate position of Case 10, then, is not the first case to meet such a limit, but the first where that limit went unfilled and stayed genuinely unresolved.

B. Summary: The Arc in Four Stages

The change traced above falls into four stages, and together they reveal the trajectory the Reasoner’s thinking moved along.

Stage 1. Internalization (Cases 01–03): The problem of how to recognize an existing structure: From the classification boundary of an individual word (01), to the hierarchical relationship among classification criteria (02), to the hierarchy of judgment criteria not yet internalized as a whole (03): the unit of ambiguity expanded from an element to the entire structure.

Stage 2. Design (Cases 04–06): A shift to the problem of building new principles outside the boundary of the existing structure: The Reasoner’s thinking appeared to move from recognizing the existing structure to designing outside its boundary. This shift was not confirmed immediately in Case 04. It was left pending, since it still had to be verified whether the shift was a single-case coincidence or whether the same pattern would repeat in the next case. Confirmation came only once that pattern actually repeated, as the Reasoner moved from filling a gap in the guideline’s scope of application (04) to constructing a basis of judgment independently and checking it against design intent, with no external source to build on (05). From there the same direction advanced further, deriving a principle from outside the hierarchy after exhausting it systematically (06).

Stage 3. Meta-Evaluation (Cases 07–08): A move to the problem of evaluating the quality of judgment itself: The level rose from choosing a tool, to applying it in a certain order (07), to evaluating in hindsight how conservative the evidence path had been (08).

Stage 4. Diagnosis (Cases 09–10): A move to the problem of examining the hierarchy itself, along with the external standard authoritative source it rests on. The level rose from defining the hierarchy as a whole (09) to diagnosing the limits of that authority (10): two separate directions, one facing inward to the hierarchy, the other outward to what it rests on.

In summary, the unit of ambiguity rose in a single direction: starting from the classification of an individual element (Internalization), through building new principles outside the boundary of the existing structure (Design) and revisiting the quality of judgment itself (Meta-Evaluation), to defining the hierarchy itself and diagnosing the limits of the standard authoritative source it rests on (Diagnosis). This rise was not a path designed in advance. The evidence is the trace itself: at each transition, a determination was carried forward into the next case rather than settled on the spot as an extension or a departure. Just as the suspected departure in Case 04 was confirmed only once it repeated in Case 05, each step up this trajectory was acknowledged only after the next case actually repeated the same pattern.

3.2 What Stayed Open

Read in case order, the open questions show how a question that arose in one case resurfaces and links to the next. Sometimes the same question sharpens into a clearer pattern through a different instance; sometimes questions that arose independently in different cases turn out to share the same structure and merge. Like the Calibration trajectory, this connection was not designed in advance: it emerged from deciding, case by case, whether each new question linked back to an earlier one.

A. Open-Question Tracking

In Case 01, the originating question arose: whether a structural gap exists where the English-based annotation guideline fails to address the functional branching of Korean spoken utterances. In Case 02, this question rose a level, from “where is the gap” to “does the judgment criterion that fills it (the framework hierarchy) hold consistently across other cases as well.” A new question arose alongside it: whether a conservative, self-formed judgment had in fact been valid, given that it was made while reference points were mixed amid guideline changes, with no later point of comparison (feedback or an announcement) available.

The question from Case 01 about a structural gap in the guideline took on a clearer pattern in Case 03, through a new instance: a gap between Korean orthography and pronunciation, combined with the speaker’s own lack of awareness of it. A new question also arose for the first time in Case 03: the difficulty of verifying the scope of a self-formed criterion built to fill such a gap. Recording the grounds for a judgment does not resolve this self-referential structure: the one who sets the criterion and the one who verifies it are the same. Here, the Reasoner’s own language habits became the specific reason that boundary blurred. This was the first form of a pattern that would recur in every case that followed.

The question from Case 01 about a structural gap in the guideline took on a clearer pattern once more in Case 04, through a case where the very definition of a neologism was unclear. Case 04 also reproduced the self-referential structure from Case 03 directly for the first time. The Reasoner set a criterion to divide two processing paths: one checking whether the word appeared in an authoritative source, the other falling back on a self-formed criterion when it did not. But whether that criterion also covered cases meeting both conditions at once (slang that is also non-standard) was left uncertain.

In Case 05, the question from Case 01 about a structural gap in the guideline was reinforced by two instances at once within a single case, each marking a point the guideline left unaddressed: the need to carry English-language conventions for non-verbal vocalization over to Korean, and a feedback pattern revealing that tagging and transcription demanded different standards of precision. Case 03’s self-referential structure was confirmed again, and a new question arose as well: how far the guideline’s own broad allowance (a judgment is acceptable if its context is reasonable) should extend. It also became clear that this input (a non-verbal vocalization) fell under neither of the two paths Case 04 had set up. For the first time, this case confirmed that some inputs fall entirely outside the existing classification system.

In Case 06, the question from Case 05 about reasonable context resurfaced. It was partially resolved under low-severity conditions, but the processing principle for high-severity cases remained undesigned. A new question from Case 04 (how to judge an input that satisfies several criteria at once) was raised as a possible pattern for the first time, as the structural similarity between Case 04 and Case 06 became clear. In reaching the judgment, the two grounds behind it (formal similarity or functional equivalence) were left unsorted. Leaving them unsorted raised a further question: whether a conclusion and its grounds should be checked separately. The question from Case 01 about the structural gap recurred for a fifth time.

In Case 07, the question from Case 04 about how to judge an input that satisfies several criteria took a more specific form: which criterion to apply first depending on speaker context. This moved it closer to a confirmed pattern. The question about reasonable context carried over from Cases 05 and 06 resolved the same way again, only under low-severity conditions, with the high-severity principle still undesigned. In the same case, a new question arose: which of two levels within the hierarchy should be applied first.

In Case 08, a new question arose about when borrowing a principle from an adjacent level of the hierarchy is justified. Another arose alongside it: among external sources of equal authority but different form (dictionary entry status or official orthography rules), which should be checked first.

In Case 09, the Reasoner organized the unifying principle for the first time: the judgment criteria built separately, case by case, all reach the same question, whether to normalize or transcribe as heard. But reaching that shared question presupposes one thing: when a new input arrives, it must already be settled which criterion to route it to first. No such routing rule yet existed. At this point it was confirmed that Case 04’s question about judging an input that meets several criteria was itself an instance of this very routing problem. In the same case, several questions arose together: whether a criterion built for an input of a single element can be carried over, without verification, to an input composed of several elements (e.g., a compound loanword); how far grounding a judgment in measurable external evidence is achievable and necessary in practice; and, when several independent lines of external evidence point to different conclusions, which should take priority.

In Case 10, the question from Case 01 about a structural gap in the guideline recurred for a ninth time. A pattern already confirmed in Case 03 appeared again in the same form: that an orthography-pronunciation gap combined with the speaker’s own lack of awareness structurally produces more cases of unclear classification. Here a stretch arose where the external norm itself (the rule the authoritative source sets) admitted more than one valid answer, and the input (audio signal) alone was not enough to distinguish them; context or speaker intent had to settle it. In the same case, a new question arose: when more than one answer is admissible, which should take priority. One principle chooses an answer that holds either way without committing to which answer is correct; the other infers the intended answer from additional clues and commits to it. This priority problem has the same structure as the one Case 09 raised, about which to prioritize when several independent lines of external evidence point to different conclusions. The difference is what conflicts: in Case 09, external sources (press frequency or quasi-official sources); here, judgment principles (leaving the answer open or inferring it).

B. Summary: The Arc in Three Stages

Reading the list of open questions in case order, the very nature of the questions shifts.

  • Early phase (01–03): Questions at the level of individual classification judgments. Which classification a single word belongs to (01); whether that classification criterion takes priority over another (02); whether a criterion can be verified by the one who set it, the limitation of a self-referential structure (03).
  • Middle phase (04–07): Questions at the level of path decisions. Which criterion to route an input to when it satisfies several at once (04); how to handle an input with no criterion to route it to (05); whether the path-decision problem from Case 04 is in fact a recurring pattern (06); which level within the hierarchy to apply first (07).
  • Late phase (08–10): Questions at the level of meta-mechanism. When borrowing supporting evidence from an adjacent level of the hierarchy is justified (08); the path-decision problem formally named as routing problem, and, separately, which of several conflicting external sources to trust (09); which takes priority when two equally valid judgment principles conflict (10).

The rise in the unit of ambiguity shown by the Calibration trajectory in Section 3 runs parallel to the rise in newly arising open questions. As the judgment structure evolved, the questions it generated as unresolved rose in the same direction.

4 Beyond the Cases

Section 3 traced how judgment criteria shifted by linking the cases together in sequence; this section sets that sequence aside and treats the ten cases as a single body. They reveal structural findings that were reconfirmed each time in the same form (4.1): propositions at the level of design, concerning not what any individual case judged but what made those judgments possible or constrained them. They also reveal questions that case-by-case judgment cannot answer (4.2).

4.1 Structural Observations

A. Capability Independence

Fluency in a language and the ability to apply a judgment procedure for handling that language are separate capabilities. Korean fluency did not guarantee the ability to apply the annotation guideline, and native fluency likewise did not guarantee the ability to discern the orthography–pronunciation boundary in one’s own spoken language. Knowing a guideline and internalizing the hierarchy by which it is applied were also confirmed to be separate capabilities. Every time, the same thing was reconfirmed: capabilities that appear adjacent on the surface must in fact be verified independently.

B. The Dependency Structure of Consistency

What annotation consistency actually depends on turned out not to be the guideline document but the framework the Reasoner had personally internalized. This dependency does not arise from any shortfall in the Reasoner’s capability. It arises from the structure of the guideline itself: when a guideline does not specify its hierarchy explicitly, the burden falls on the Annotator to reconstruct that hierarchy in real time, and this burden directly affects judgment consistency. If so, the dependency is not a variable to be filled by the Reasoner’s capability, but one that needs to be controlled by a procedure, such as verifying a judgment against external evidence. What that procedure needs to verify was narrowed further in another case. A guideline conveys a processing conclusion but does not include a procedure for verifying whether the premise behind that conclusion holds. Where that verification is left to the Annotator’s own initiative, a false premise can pass through unfiltered, and the conclusion drawn from it can be applied as is, leading to misclassification. Annotation consistency, in other words, is not guaranteed by the accuracy of the guideline document alone. It also depends on how fully the Annotator has internalized the procedure of verifying the premises behind a conclusion.

C. The Separation of Validity and Verification

Having reached a judgment is not the same as having verified it. Securing grounds is different from resolving uncertainty. Grounds may do nothing to eliminate the uncertainty; they may only justify a decision to accept the risk and proceed.
The same holds for confirming that an inferred criterion aligns with a self-formed judgment principle. That alignment is not proof that the inference was correct. It is a coherence check, and only for the point in time at which it was made.
This separation applies to the path a conclusion takes as well. Because the order in which several judgment criteria are applied can itself change the conclusion, the validity of the conclusion and the validity of the order that led to it must be verified independently. The same is true of the search for external evidence: even among sources of equal authority, focusing on one familiar form can leave more direct evidence in another form unnoticed. Sufficiency is not guaranteed by the authority level of the sources alone. It also depends on how varied the forms searched were.

D. The Separation of Completion and Guarantee

Completing the work of handling a case does not guarantee the outcome of the downstream stage where a model uses its results. That transcription has been processed does not guarantee that a model can learn meaning from the data: whether the orthographic and semantic aspects of a word were already separated by design at the data-construction stage remains unverified by the transcription work itself. Where the previous three observations concern the Reasoner’s individual capability and procedure, this one points to a gap that opens when the work passes down to model training.

4.2 Out-of-Scope Questions

A. The Prior-Design Dimension

Stretches that case-by-case judgment cannot resolve arose repeatedly within the portfolio: the external norm allows more than one valid answer, or the input alone does not settle the classification. Each time, the response was to select an individual processing principle after the fact. One question remains beyond the reach of case-by-case judgment: whether a design-level solution is possible for these stretches as a whole (an alternative to the data-labeling methodology itself, or a proactive policy at the level of the criteria designer).

B. The Legitimacy of Principle Transfer

The same limit appears when an input fits nowhere in the judgment hierarchy built up across several cases: at what point it is justified to infer and apply the logic of an adjacent level. Individual cases confirmed one principle: even when two paths reach the same conclusion, whether the evidence was direct or borrowed from an adjacent level must be established separately from the conclusion itself. But the criterion for when such borrowing should be allowed lies beyond the scope of case-by-case judgment.

C. Designing the Threshold for Evidence Adoption

Grounding a judgment in measurable external evidence is also a matter of deciding a threshold: how much evidence is enough. The same threshold problem resurfaces in a different form elsewhere in the portfolio. When more than one answer is admissible, one principle chooses an answer that holds either way without committing to which is correct (Abstain), while another infers the intended answer from additional clues and commits to it (Evidence-based Reconstruction). Where these two conflict, the question is how strong that supporting signal must be before it counts as evidence. Different in form, both questions converge on the same design problem: how much evidence must accumulate before it earns the right to change a conclusion. How to design that threshold (including how much is actually necessary for the purposes of data training) is a matter of cost against benefit, and cannot be settled by the judgment of any individual case alone.

5 Candidate Connections to Physical AI

Unpacked at the level of mechanism rather than as individual speculations, the Physical AI Implications from the ten cases sort into six types, differing in how often they recur. The first four (A–D) concern how mechanisms confirmed in the Utterance Project transfer into Physical AI’s behavioral and safety decisions; the latter two (E–F) concern how those same mechanisms transfer into the infrastructure and judgment systems used to build and verify Physical AI systems. All six remain tagged unverified. Without separate empirical Physical AI data, these connections cannot move past the stage of speculation.

  • Type A. Direct Physical-Action Transfer from Misclassification or Non-Recognition (Cases 01, 02, 04, 05, 09): the speculation that misclassifying or failing to recognize a word or sound could lead to an unintended physical action or a command failure. Recurring in five cases, this is the most frequent type.
  • Type B. Asymmetry in the Safety Threshold of Fallback Principles (Cases 05, 06, 10): in the Utterance domain, a fallback principle was applied as a last resort when every other judgment criterion was blocked, and that processing was judged low-risk on the reasoning that whichever way it went, the meaning of the utterance stayed the same. The speculation is that if this principle carries over to Physical AI without a boundary on how far it can be applied, the same risk assessment could lead to a safety incident, and severity would have to be reassessed when the domain changes.
  • Type C. Transfer of the Impact of Order- and Timing-Dependency in Judgment (Cases 07, 08): in the Utterance domain, the order in which several judgment criteria were applied, or the point at which an external standard changed, could each alter the conclusion. The consequence stayed on the screen: a wrong output, and nothing more. The speculation is that in Physical AI, the same difference could translate directly into a physical action.
  • Type D. Transfer to Multilingual Named-Entity Handling in Locale and Cultural Conversion Judgments (Cases 06, 09): the speculation that the method of judging an input by what it is functionally equivalent to, when every predefined classification criterion fails to fit, and the judgment of whether to render a loanword in Korean or keep it in the original script, could extend to handling cross-linguistic named entities such as place names and personal names in multilingual, multicultural environments.
  • Type E. Transfer into the Design of Data and Verification Infrastructure (Cases 02, 08, 09): four findings recur here. Annotating a word accurately does not guarantee functional learnability, whether a model can learn how that word operates within context and speaker intent. The others are the misjudgment risk of drawing on indirect evidence, the absence of any given hierarchy among external sources, and the medium gap between offline text processing and a real-time voice interface: whether judgment happens on transcribed material or on the audio signal itself, and whether there is time to verify before deciding or the decision must be immediate. The speculation is that all four findings are not model behavioral errors but design problems in how the infrastructure behind Physical AI is built and verified.
  • Type F. Design Precedents for Structural Flaws in the Judgment System Itself (Cases 03, 06): the speculation that two cases (one where the judgment hierarchy was never internalized, another where every level of the hierarchy was applied and none fit) could each serve as a precedent to examine before setting design principles for Physical AI.
